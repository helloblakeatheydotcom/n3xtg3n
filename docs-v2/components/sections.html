Sections

Use sections to break larger panel content into smaller, scannable groups with optional rails, supporting metadata, and collapsible structure.

Basic, meta, form, and grouped sections Rails, nested cards, and panel-friendly structure Anatomy, interaction, token reference

Overview

Sections break larger panel content into smaller, scannable domains. They create internal hierarchy inside a panel without introducing another full panel shell, and they work well for present levels, grouped field sets, related records, and supporting content areas.

Core Structure

Live inside panelsSections are intended to organize content inside larger panel bodies, not replace panels as page-level containers.

Header is requiredEvery section has a header with a title and a consistent top structure for optional supporting meta and a collapse toggle.

Content is requiredThe content region holds the section’s primary copy, fields, cards, or related grouped items.

Related content is optionalUse the related-content area when cards or records need clear separation from the primary field content.

Section Behavior

Static or collapsibleSections may stay open by default or collapse independently inside the surrounding panel.

Rails communicate groupingRail color differentiates domains or categories, not interaction state or selection.

Meta stays secondarySupporting meta should remain quieter than the title while still giving useful scan-first context.

Hierarchy stays lightSections should feel lighter-weight than panels so the panel remains the dominant container.

Guidance

Use sections to organize related content inside a panel when a single panel body would otherwise become too long, too mixed, or harder to scan.

How To Use Sections

Group related domains, field clusters, or supporting records into sections so each subsection reads as a clear unit.

Use sections when the content needs internal hierarchy but does not justify another full panel shell.

Keep section titles short and specific so the user can scan the larger panel quickly.

Use supporting meta only when it adds immediate context such as counts, status, or recent update information.

Section Rules

Do not use sections as a substitute for page-level or panel-level layout structure.

Keep header and content padding consistent across all section variants.

Use the related-content divider only when secondary cards or records need separation from the main fields.

Field groups inside a section need enough vertical spacing to read clearly without competing with the surrounding panel rhythm.

Variants

Use the examples below to compare the common section patterns: basic, supporting-meta, form-content, and grouped sections inside a panel.

Basic Section

Use a basic section to subdivide content within a larger panel while keeping a lighter hierarchy than a full panel header. When no rail variant is applied, the section uses the default slate rail.

Use sections when a panel needs internal organization without introducing another full panel shell. Sections can group copy, fields, and related records.

Section With Supporting Meta

Supporting meta gives quick secondary context such as active goal count or recency, while keeping the title as the primary anchor.

Supporting meta is optional and should remain quieter than the title. It works best for concise context that helps the reader decide whether to expand the section.

Section With Form Fields

Sections commonly group a short set of related fields and related cards inside a larger panel or workflow.

Section Group Inside Panel

Sections are most often used inside panels to create clear internal structure across multiple related domains or content areas.

Present Levels of Performance

Sections can stack cleanly inside a panel body without introducing another panel border or footer treatment.

Use the default slate rail when no category color is needed. Add rail variants only when they help differentiate domains or categories without relying on full surface color shifts.

Anatomy

A section creates lighter-weight hierarchy inside a panel. It uses a required header and content region, with optional supporting meta, rail color, collapse behavior, and related content separation.

The header and content region are required. Supporting meta, the collapse toggle, rail color, and the related-content area are optional depending on how much hierarchy and support the section needs.

Parts

Rail

Optional color accent that helps distinguish domains or categories inside a larger panel.

Header

Required top region that contains the title and the consistent placement of optional supporting meta and a collapse toggle.

Title

Primary label that anchors the section and keeps the larger panel easy to scan.

Content

Required content region that holds copy, form fields, records, or other grouped items.

Related content area

Optional secondary area separated by a divider when cards, goals, or records need visual distinction from the main content.

Interaction

Sections may be static or collapsible, but they should always behave as lighter-weight internal structure inside the surrounding panel.

Collapsible Behavior

Use a real button for collapsible section headers.

Keep aria-expanded and aria-controls in sync with the content region.

Collapsed sections hide only their own content, not the surrounding panel.

When sections are static, keep the same header structure without the collapse interaction.

Visual Behavior

The left rail should align with the section content area rather than the outer panel border.

Use the default slate rail unless a variant is applied for meaningful domain grouping.

Rail color should communicate grouping, not selection or active state.

Meta text should remain secondary but readable, and fields or cards inside a section must stay keyboard reachable when expanded.
