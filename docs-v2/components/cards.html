Cards

Use cards for lightweight summaries, related records, and compact content groups that need clear boundaries without the stronger hierarchy of a panel.

Overview

Cards are lightweight containers for grouped summaries, related records, and short supporting content. They provide structure and separation, but they should remain lighter than panels and avoid taking on page-level layout responsibilities.

Core Structure

Single lightweight containerCards use one compact surface with subtle border, shadow, and internal spacing.

Title and body are the common patternOptional eyebrow, metadata, badges, and footer actions support the summary when needed.

Actions stay explicitEdit, delete, and secondary actions belong on visible buttons, not on the full card surface.

Works alone or in groupsCards can stand alone or appear in stacks and grids when multiple summaries need equal visual weight.

Card Behavior

Static by defaultCards do not collapse or expand. They present summary information and direct the user to explicit actions.

Hover emphasizes the boundaryHover and focus strengthen the border and reveal the accent rail without tinting the full surface.

Selection is optionalOnly use selected styling when cards participate in a selectable set or support bulk actions.

Metadata stays secondaryMetadata, badges, and footer actions should support the summary rather than compete with the title and body.

Guidance

Use cards when information needs lightweight grouping and a clear boundary, but does not need the stronger hierarchy, header, or layout responsibilities of a panel.

How To Use Cards

Use cards for summaries, related records, assessments, services, goals, and overview content that can be read quickly.

Keep card content compact. If the content becomes long-form, deeply structured, or form-heavy, move it to a panel or a dedicated page section.

Use metadata for secondary facts like dates, counts, frequency, or service details when those details support scanning.

Use footer actions only when the card needs a single clear next step after the summary content.

Card Rules

Do not use cards as a substitute for a page section or panel when the content needs stronger hierarchy or grouped form layout.

Keep actions explicit and visible. The full card should not behave like an edit trigger.

Use badges and decorative rails sparingly so they reinforce status or grouping rather than overwhelm the content.

When cards appear in a grid, keep their visual weight consistent so the layout feels balanced and scan-friendly.

Anatomy

A card is a single lightweight container that may include contextual eyebrow text, visible actions, body content, metadata, and an optional footer action region.

The container, title, and body define the common card pattern. Eyebrow text, visible actions, metadata, badges, rail emphasis, and footer actions are optional depending on the record type.

Parts

Container

Defines the lightweight surface, spacing, border, and subtle elevation for the card.

Rail

Optional accent revealed on hover, focus, or selection to strengthen emphasis without tinting the full surface.

Eyebrow

Optional contextual label above the title for category or content framing.

Title

Primary label that anchors the summary and keeps the card easy to scan.

Body

Primary summary content, supporting copy, or lightweight structured information.

Metadata

Optional secondary facts such as dates, frequency, score, or provider details.

Actions

Optional explicit icon buttons or a footer action used for editing, deleting, or taking a next step.

Interaction

Cards are static containers. Interaction should stay attached to explicit actions, links, or selection behavior rather than turning the entire card into a generic click target.

Hover and Selection

Hover and focus strengthen the card border and reveal the left rail to indicate affordance without changing the full card background.

Use selected state only when the card participates in a selectable set or supports batch actions.

Do not use hover treatment to imply that every card is clickable when the only interactive elements are internal actions.

When cards appear in groups, keep hover and selection treatment consistent across the full set.

Action Behavior

Keep edit and delete actions visible and keyboard reachable at all times.

Use semantic headings and clear button labels so screen reader users can distinguish cards inside repeated lists or grids.

Footer actions should remain lightweight and limited in count so the card still reads primarily as summary content.

Use inline links sparingly inside card body copy, and make sure they remain visually secondary to the card title.
